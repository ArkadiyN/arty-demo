## Summary

This open-access PMC study fired two types of steel projectiles (spherical ball bearings and chisel-nosed fragment-simulating projectiles) at isolated swine ribs across a wide velocity range (99–1265 m/s) to characterize bone perforation thresholds. Bone perforation occurred at velocities as low as 99 m/s with kinetic energy densities as low as 0.14 J/mm², well below common fixed-threshold assumptions. Fragment shape matters significantly: chisel-nosed FSPs transferred 1.5–2× more energy to bone than equal-mass spheres, producing more secondary bone fragments that pose independent vascular injury risk.

## Key Findings

- **Minimum perforation velocity** (ball bearing, 0.68 g, 5.5 mm dia.): **99 m/s**
- **Minimum kinetic energy density for perforation**: **0.14 J/mm²** (KED = KE / cross-sectional area of projectile)
- Spherical projectiles (BBs) cause less bone damage per unit KE than chisel-nosed FSPs due to shape-dependent energy transfer
- At **>551 m/s** (KED >6.44 J/mm²), bone transitions from ductile failure (elastic deformation then perforation) to **brittle fracture** with multiple secondary fragment ejection
- Secondary bone fragments from high-velocity hits risk damage to adjacent vascular structures — a lethality mechanism not captured by simple KE thresholds
- Chisel-nosed FSPs deposited approximately **1.5–2× more energy** into bone than same-velocity ball bearings
- Prior human bone perforation literature cited ~61 m/s as a threshold, but projectile type was unspecified; this study clarifies the mass/shape dependency

## Extracted Content

### Experimental Setup

- **Projectiles**: (1) Steel ball bearings (BBs): 0.68 g, 5.5 mm diameter; (2) Chisel-nosed FSPs (CN FSPs): 1.10 g
- **Launching system**: Cartridge-based (Enfield Number 3 proof housing)
- **Stand-off distance**: 3 m, laser-guided accuracy
- **Velocity measurement**: High-speed videography at 21,005 fps
- **Post-impact analysis**: X-radiography + micro-CT scanning
- **Sample sizes**: 20 BB impacts, 15 CN FSP impacts

### Velocity and Failure Mode Regimes

| Velocity Range             | Projectile | Failure Mode                                                  |
| -------------------------- | ---------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 99–323 m/s                 | BB         | Ductile: elastic deformation → plastic failure → perforation  |
| 180–217 m/s                | CN FSP     | Ductile failure with increased bone damage                    |
| >551 m/s (KED >6.44 J/mm²) | BB         | Brittle: instantaneous fracture, multiple secondary fragments |

### Key Quotations

> "The slowest successful impact velocity was 99 m/s, with bones experiencing perforation at kinetic energy density (KED) as low as 0.14 J/mm²."

> "Energy transfer to the bone was greater for the CN FSPs, resulting in increased bone damage and the production of secondary bone fragments."

> "Instantaneous failure occurred at the point of impact, with multiple secondary fragments exiting" at velocities >551 m/s.

> "Secondary bone fragments...may risk damage to vascular structures" — a secondary lethality pathway.

### Implications for 79 J Fixed Threshold

The 79 J criterion is a whole-fragment KE value, not a KED value. A 1 g fragment at 79 J carries KED ≈ 79 J / (π × (3.5 mm)² / 4) ≈ 2.1 J/mm² for a 7 mm sphere — well above the 0.14 J/mm² perforation floor for bone, confirming that the 79 J threshold conservatively predicts bone-penetrating capability. However, the study shows that much lower energies can still perforate bone, and that fragment shape (FSP vs. sphere) materially changes lethality at the same KE, undermining a single fixed threshold.
