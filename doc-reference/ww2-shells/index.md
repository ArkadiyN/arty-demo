# WW2-Era Artillery HE Shell Parameters for Fragmentation Lethality Modeling

**Research Date:** May 25, 2026\
**Source:** Web search via multiple technical repositories and military archives

______________________________________________________________________

## Overview

This document compiles shell geometry, explosive fill, and steel specification data for five major WW2-era artillery HE shells. Data is sourced from web-accessible military archives, technical databases, Aberdeen Proving Ground reports, and secondary historical references. Note that precise fragmentation parameters (dynamic fracture stress σ_f and Mott parameter γ) remain largely undocumented in open sources; values here are estimated from material science literature and experimental fragmentation studies.

## 2. Steel Specification and Dynamic Fracture Parameters

### 2.1 WW2 Artillery Shell Body Steel

#### General Properties

**Typical Fill Ratios and Blast Performance:**

- 19-ton steel: Limited to ~7% explosive fill
- 30-ton steel: Permitted ~15% explosive fill (improved blast; reduced fragmentation)
- Late 1944 improvement: RDX/TNT adoption for anti-personnel fragmentation (≈25% improvement)

**Fragmentation Characteristics:**

- Shell wall rupture produces three fragment sprays: nose, side, and base
- Fragment velocities: 1370–2740 m/s (4500–9000 ft/s)
- Fragment density concentrated near burst axis, decreases with distance (≈55% loss between 5–37 m)

**Source:** [Defense and Freedom: Fragmentation effects of artillery HE fires](https://defense-and-freedom.blogspot.com/2021/10/fragmentation-effects-of-artillery-he.html); [FRAGMENTATION EFFECTS OF THE 75MM H. E. SHELL T3 (M48) - DTIC Archive](https://archive.org/details/DTIC_AD0702233)

### 2.2 Dynamic Fracture Parameters for Shell Steel

#### Mott Fragmentation Theory (1943–1947)

**Principal Investigator:** N.F. Mott (University of Bristol; later Fort Halstead, UK)

**Core Mechanism:**

- Fracture initiates at statistical "weak points" in the material
- Stress-release waves (Mott waves) propagate from each fracture site
- Fragment size determined by distance traveled before stress relief
- Statistical distribution of weak points produces log-normal/Weibull fragment size distribution

**Fragmentation Parameter (Mott β / γ):**

- Empirically relates fragment mass distribution to material properties
- Weakly correlated with grain diameter and fragmentation resistance
- No absolute universal value; varies with alloy, grain size, temperature, and strain rate

**Key Papers:**

- Mott, N.F. (1943–1944). Internal Ministry of Supply reports (UK)
- Mott, N.F. (1947). "Fragmentation of shell cases." *Proceedings of the Royal Society*, A190, 300–308

**Source:** [A Theory of Fragmentation - ResearchGate](https://researchgate.net/publication/227145441_A_Theory_of_Fragmentation); [Fragmentation of Expanding Cylinders and the Statistical Theory of N. F. Mott - ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/252256638_Fragmentation_of_Expanding_Cylinders_and_the_Statistical_Theory_of_N_F_Mott); [Fragmentation of shell cases - Royal Society](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspa.1947.0042)

______________________________________________________________________

#### Grady–Kipp Dynamic Fragmentation Model

**Applications:** High strain-rate materials science; used in modern ballistic codes (e.g., CTH hydrocode)

**Material Classes:** Solid spall (fracture-toughness dominated), solid spall (flow-stress dominated), liquid spall (melt/vaporization)

**Weibull Fitting (Example):** k=1032, m=9.5 (fitted to cylinder fragmentation experiments with explosive fill)

**Advantage over Mott:** Physically-based (incorporates strain rate, fracture toughness, yield stress); better for complex geometries

**Limitation:** Requires material-property input (yield σ_y, fracture toughness K_IC, density ρ); WW2 shell steel data sparse

**Source:** [Grady–Kipp dynamic fragmentation theory - ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167663604000043); [IMPACT FRAGMENTATION - USRA/LPSC](https://www.hou.usra.edu/meetings/lpsc2017/pdf/1295.pdf)

______________________________________________________________________

#### Dynamic Yield Strength and Fracture Stress

**General Findings (Modern Materials):**

- Dynamic yield strength of mild steel: varies 300–400 MPa at strain rates 10^3–10^4 s^-1
- High-strength armor steels (hardened): 900–1200 MPa yield; reduced ductility (brittle fracture risk)
- Fracture stress under explosive loading: highly dependent on temperature, strain rate, and microstructure

**WW2 Shell Steel (Estimated):**

- Yield stress σ_y: ~250–350 MPa (annealed/normalized 19-ton steel; typical for WW2 HE shells)
- Dynamic fracture stress σ_f: unknown; literature suggests 400–600 MPa for similar materials under ~10^3 s^-1 strain rate
- Elongation at fracture: ~10–20% (typical for normalized medium-carbon steel)

**Caveat:** Exact values for WW2-specific alloys are not documented in open sources. Modern data on similar grades is used as proxy.

**Source:** [Dynamic Yield Strength of Mild Steel under Impact Loading - ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/267795016_Dynamic_Yield_Strength_of_Mild_Steel_under_Impact_Loading); [Dynamic Fracture and Fragmentation Characteristics - MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/1996-1944/13/3/778); [Johnson-Cook Flow and Fracture Model - ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0143974X22003741)

## 5. Recommendations for Model Implementation

### For Lethality Fragment Distribution

1. **Use Mott (1947) theory** as primary fragmentation model:

   - Requires only: shell mass, explosive mass, shell caliber, steel yield strength
   - Produces log-normal fragment size distribution
   - Parameter γ estimated from material grain size or fitted to test data if available

1. **When available, use Grady–Kipp** for strain-rate effects:

   - More physically accurate but requires full material card (σ_y, K_IC, ρ, strain-rate hardening)
   - Overkill for WW2 ordnance without detailed metallurgical data

1. **Proxy Fragmentation Parameters** (pending primary data):

   - Assume ~350 MPa yield stress for normalized 19-ton shell steel
   - Assume ~8–12% explosive fill ratio for typical WW2 HE shells (varies by caliber)
   - Estimate Mott γ ≈ 2.5–3.5 based on typical medium-carbon steel (grain size D ≈ 50–100 μm)

### For Cross-Validation

- Compare fragment count vs. distance predictions with **DTIC AD0702233 data** for 75mm M48 (available as reference curve)
- Check fill ratios: range 7–15% across all nations; US and British on low end, German higher
